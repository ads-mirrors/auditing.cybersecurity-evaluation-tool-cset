Overview and Scope of the IMR

The IMR is based on the principle that a resilient incident management function can improve an organization's overall cyber resilience. The IMR consists of a series of questions, the answers to which provide insights into how an organization can improve its ability to identify, analyze, and respond to incidents in a repeatable manner.

An “Incident Management Function” is that part(s) of the organization that provides incident handling and response services for the organization. It is responsible for the performance of all incident handling and response practices. In this report, for the sake of simplicity, the term “incident management personnel” refers to the groups or individuals who perform the Incident Management Function activities. Further, the term “organization” refers to the entire [sovereign] group, including the incident management personnel. We use the term “stakeholders” to identify those with a vested interest in the organization's Incident Management Function or in its activities, whether internal or external to the organization.

The IMR consists of a structured, facilitated interview of key personnel, and takes approximately a half-day to complete. The IMR helps an organization understand and qualitatively measure its incident handling and response capability and the cybersecurity practices that support it. Key personnel are asked to describe how selected practices for both incident handling and response and cybersecurity are performed and managed. Participants are asked to articulate evidence regarding the performance of these practices. The IMR examines Incident Management practices grouped in the following categories.

  1. Event Detection and Handling
  2. Incident Declaration, Handling and Response
  3. Post-Incident Analysis and Testing
  4. Integrate Organizational Capabilities
  5. Protect and Sustain the Incident Management Function
  6. Prepare for Incident Response

The practices examined during an IMR are derived from a set of metrics developed in 2002 by the Defense Information Systems Agency (DISA) and National Security Agency (NSA) and called the DoD Computer Network Defense Service Provider (CNDSP) Evaluator Scoring Metrics (ESM). In 2003, the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) and United States Computer Emergency Readiness Team (US-CERT) funded initial work for the CERT Division at Carnegie Mellon University's Software Engineering Institute to adapt that set of metrics for Federal use into a document called the Federal Incident Response Evaluation (FIRE) Capabilities. Additional practices come from the NIST Cybersecurity Framework, NIST SP 800-61 Computer Security Incident Handling Guide, the Cyber Resilience Review (CRR) and from a larger security and business continuity framework known as the CERT Resilience Management Model (CERT-RMM).


About this Report

The answers gathered from your key staff have been collected and evaluated, and are presented in this report.

The report summarizes the assessment findings, and provides your organization with options for consideration for each practice. The options for consideration provide general guidelines or activities intended to help your organization improve its incident handling and response capabilities. These options are not meant to fully represent all activities needed for a robust incident management program but, rather, to provide initial guidance on how to incorporate various incident handling and response practices, including National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), CERT Resilience Management Model (CERT-RMM), and other standards. Detailed information about CERT-RMM can be found at www.cert.org/resilience. The options for consideration provided include guidance from NIST Special Publications. The primary audience for these documents is United States Federal Civilian Agencies, however NIST encourages the adoption of these guidelines by State, local, and tribal governments, as well as private sector organizations. Guidance from the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (NIST CSF) for Improving Critical Infrastructure Cybersecurity is also included. The Framework, created through collaboration between industry and government, consists of standards, guidelines, and practices to promote the protection of critical infrastructure.


Incident Management Review Results

The IMR is an interview-based assessment. It is understood that participants often do not have complete knowledge of an organization's operations. Actual performance may vary from what is indicated in this report. Organizational performance is presented across several dimensions within the report. Scores are provided for individual Practices, Goals, and Domains.
