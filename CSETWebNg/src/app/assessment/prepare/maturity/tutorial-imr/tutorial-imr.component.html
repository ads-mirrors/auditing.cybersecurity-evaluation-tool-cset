Incident Management Review (IMR) Tutorial

Overview

The Incident Management Review (IMR) was developed by the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) and is based on the principle that an effective incident management function can enhance the overall cyber resilience of an organization. An incident management function is responsible for detecting and responding to incidents that impact an organization's critical services.

Within the assessment, “incident management personnel” refers to the groups or individuals who perform the incident management function activities, while “organization” refers to the entire entity, including these personnel. “Stakeholders” refers to those with an interest in the organization's incident management function or its activities, whether internal or external to the organization.

The scope of this assessment focuses on the critical services overseen by the incident management function and the assets necessary to provide those services. For evaluating a single critical service, the Cyber Resilience Review (CRR) should be completed.

Critical Services

A critical service is defined as a set of activities an organization carries out in the performance of a duty or in the production of a product that is so critical to the organization's success that its disruption would severely impact continued operations or success in meeting the organization's mission.

Organizations typically have a set of critical services that define their mission. The focus on those critical services for the assessment — rather than assessing the organization as a whole — helps to scope the assessment and tie the results to the organization's mission.

Below are some examples of organizations and their typical critical services:

  • Banks and other financial institutions: clearing and settlement, mortgage application processing
  • Emergency services providers: processing 911 calls, dispatch
  • Electrical power plants: electricity generation, electricity distribution
  • Hospitals: clinical services, prescription management
  • Government agencies: court case management, benefit management
  • Manufacturing companies: machining operations, product engineering and design
  • Airports: air traffic control, fuel management
Asset Types

Like the CRR assessment, the IMR considers four categories of assets: People, Information, Technology, and Facilities. In the IMR assessment, any question asking about assets is referring to all the asset types and must be answered considering all the various asset types combined. Unlike the CRR, the IMR assessment does not have questions that ask about each asset type individually. The definition for each asset type is listed below:

  • People: the staff (both internal and external to the organization) such as people that support data centers or otherwise use information and communications technology to operate, monitor, and support the service.
  • Information: account information, technology asset configuration files, operational data, customer information, and other information necessary for the delivery of a service.
  • Technology: computers (hardware), software, control systems, or other technology including external information systems used by the organization to deliver the service.
  • Facilities: office buildings, data centers, and other physical structures supporting the delivery of the service.
Assessment
The IMR assessment consists of 88 practices (questions), each aligning with a goal associated with one of the six domains:
  • Event Detection and Handling
  • Incident Declaration, Handling, and Response
  • Post-Incident Analysis and Testing
  • Integrate Organizational Capabilities
  • Protect and Sustain the Incident Management Function
  • Prepare for Incident Response

Each practice includes the following components.

Domain

Each domain has a set of goals with associated practices.

Goal

The intended incident management outcome that each of its associated practices aims to enable.

Identifier

Each question has an assigned practice identifier structured as follows:

Domain [DD]: Goal Number [G#]. Question Number [Q#]

An example of the above structure is IR:G1.Q1, which represents Incident Declaration, Handling and Response: Goal 1. Question Number 1.

Practice

Questions designed to evaluate if the organization has implemented the associated goal.

CSET Icons
Selecting the Supplemental Guidance icon provides information on:
  • Question Intent
  • Criteria for “Yes” Response
  • Criteria for “Incomplete” Response
Answer Buttons
Select the applicable answer for each question. Unanswered questions are calculated as a “No” response.
  • Yes - the organization fully performs the activity specified in the question.
  • Incomplete - the organization partially performs the activity.
  • No - the organization does not perform the activity at all.
